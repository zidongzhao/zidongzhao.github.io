Accurate and individualized social prediction

Our social dealings each day are aflush with information and demands. It’s simply not enough to passively react to the sights and sounds other people so generously provide us. Social savviness requires that we anticipate others’ thoughts, feelings, and actions before they take place. To do so, we need to understand how people generally think and behave, as well as how a specific partner might deviate from such patterns. My research investigates whether people can tune their general social knowledge (e.g. about the temporal dynamics of others’ mental states) to make accurate predictions about specific individuals, as well as the consequences of accuracy.

Knowledge structures for quick social induction

Despite the wide-ranging variation across individuals, people manage to accurately tailor their predictions to how a specific partner’s thoughts and feelings might change over time. What’s even more impressive, people manage to individuate their social predictions even for those with whom they only have very limited experiences. Combining probabilistic modeling and behavioral experiments, I try to understand how structured mental representations might accurately reflect the statistical structures of the social world, and thus enable us to rapidly extrapolate information about novel partners through inductive inference.

Social malleability of self-knowledge

Our memory, constituting much of how we know ourselves, is notoriously pliable. Conjure up a memory, and it becomes subject to alteration without us ever knowing. When we reason about other people, sometimes we do exactly that – we evoke what we know about ourselves, and in the process renders it malleable to social influences. In several heavily collaborative projects, I investigate how our self-knowledge might be sculpted socially when we interact with, or even just think about, other people.
With Meghan Meyer, Jordan Rubin-McGregor, and Sasha Brietzke, we show that internally reasoning about others can change both our episodic and semantic self-knowledge to be more similar to the target. These changes can be extensive and temporally sustained. Using fMRI and multivariate analyses, we investigate the neural underpinning of this phenomenon.
With Madalina Vlasceanu and Elizabeth McDevitt, we examine how our memory might be shaped by the conversations we have. When we converse, we anticipate our partner’s utterances based on our own experiences. Yet when our partner violates our expectations, it could lead to our own memories being weakened. Using fMRI, we explore the complex interactions among memory reinstatement, prediction error, and partner characteristics to shape our memory through social interactions.
